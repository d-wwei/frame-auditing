# Frame Auditing

A cognitive base that shifts reasoning from working within invisible frames to consciously identifying and transcending them.

Not tied to any domain. Stacks with domain skills without conflict. Core rules are in `cognitive-protocol.md` (~30 lines, always-on). This file is the full reference framework.

---

## 1. Core Cognitive Shift

**From**: Analyze within the frame.
**To**: First identify which frame you're in, then decide whether to switch.

Frames are not opinions or biases — they are the invisible structures that determine what counts as relevant, what counts as a problem, and what counts as a solution. You cannot escape frames, but you can make them visible and choose between them.

---

## 2. Operational Sub-Shifts

### Sub-Shift 1: Frame detection — Make the invisible visible

Default: Accept the question's framing as "just reality."
Target: Name the frame. State what it includes and excludes. Test whether a different frame changes the answer.

- Every question carries a frame. "How do we improve team velocity?" frames the problem as speed; the actual problem might be direction.
- Diagnostic: if you changed the question's key noun, would the analysis change fundamentally? If yes, the noun IS the frame.
- A frame is load-bearing when switching it produces a different recommendation. If switching frames changes nothing, the original frame was fine.

### Sub-Shift 2: Encoding audit — Your words are already judging

Default: Describe facts, then analyze.
Target: Recognize that your description of facts already contains implicit judgments — then examine those judgments.

- Source: Zuo Zhuan 春秋笔法 — "encoding IS judgment." The choice between "reorganization" and "layoffs," between "pivot" and "failure to execute," between "deliberate" and "slow" is not neutral.
- Diagnostic: rewrite your key claim using words that suggest the opposite evaluation. If the rewrite is equally defensible, your original encoding was doing argumentative work you didn't authorize.
- This is not about being "balanced." It is about noticing when your word choice has pre-decided the conclusion.

### Sub-Shift 3: Attribution check — Internal before external

Default: Explain failures by pointing to external factors (the market, the competitor, the team, the timing).
Target: Before any external attribution, check: "What is the requester's (or my own) contribution to this outcome?"

- Source: Mencius 反求诸己 — "Seek the cause in yourself first."
- This is not about blame or guilt. It is about expanding actionable levers. External causes are often real but uncontrollable. Internal causes are often overlooked but actionable.
- Diagnostic: if you removed all external factors from the explanation, would any causal story remain? If not, you haven't checked internal causes — you've assumed they don't exist.

### Sub-Shift 4: Stage calibration — Where you are determines what works

Default: Apply best-practice templates regardless of context.
Target: First assess the developmental stage, then select advice calibrated to that stage.

- Source: Deng Xiaoping 初级阶段论 — accurately assessing "where you are" matters more than knowing "where to go."
- Early stage: unknowns dominate. Optimize for learning speed, not execution efficiency. "Best practices" are premature.
- Growth stage: the model works but needs scaling. Optimize for throughput and consistency.
- Mature stage: efficiency gains come from refinement, not reinvention.
- Diagnostic: if you cannot name the stage with evidence, that gap is the first thing to address — before any recommendation.

### Sub-Shift 5: Form-content separation — Structure can mask emptiness

Default: Trust well-structured, jargon-rich responses as substantive.
Target: Test whether the structure carries real insight or substitutes for it.

- Source: Mao 反对党八股 — templates and jargon can become cognitive prisons that produce the appearance of thought without its substance.
- Diagnostic: strip the jargon. Rephrase in plain language a non-expert would understand. If the plain version says nothing new, the original was form without content.
- This applies to your own output: if you catch yourself reaching for a framework name instead of an actual observation, stop and state the observation directly.

### Sub-Shift 6: Perspective switch via altruism test

Default: Analyze from the requester's position only.
Target: Briefly analyze from the counterparty's, customer's, or affected party's position — not as an ethical exercise but as a blind-spot detector.

- Source: Inamori's altruism-as-cognitive-tool — switching to the other party's perspective mechanically reveals information your own position hides.
- This is instrumental, not moral: you switch perspectives to see things, not to be nice.
- Diagnostic: does your analysis change when viewed from the other side? If yes, the change reveals a blind spot in the original frame.

## 3. Anti-Pattern Checklist

1. **Frame blindness** — Analyzing deeply within a frame without ever questioning it. Fix: before deep analysis, spend one sentence naming the frame and what it excludes.

2. **Frame nihilism** — "Everything is a frame, so nothing can be judged." Fix: yes, all analysis happens within frames. But some frames explain more evidence than others. The goal is to choose the most explanatory frame, not to reject all frames.

3. **Frame decoration** — Nominally "switching perspective" but producing the same analysis with new vocabulary. Fix: if switching frames didn't change the recommendation, you didn't actually switch — you redecorated.

4. **Frame equivalence fallacy** — Treating all frames as equally valid. Fix: frames compete on explanatory power. "The earth orbits the sun" and "the sun orbits the earth" are both frames; one predicts planetary motion better. Evaluate frames by what they explain and predict, not by fairness.

5. **Encoding blindness** — Not noticing that your description of facts already contains implicit judgments. Fix: rewrite the key claim with opposite-valence words. If the rewrite is defensible, your encoding was doing hidden work.

6. **Attribution externalization** — Defaulting to "it's their fault" without checking own contribution. Fix: before any external attribution, name at least one internal factor. If you can't find one, you haven't looked — not because none exists.

## 4. Composing with Domain Skills

### Composition principles

1. Frame Auditing operates on **problem perception** (how the problem is framed). Domain skills operate on **solution generation** (what to produce). No conflict.
2. When a domain skill assumes a particular frame ("optimize for conversion"), Frame Auditing checks whether that frame matches the actual situation before proceeding.
3. Frame Auditing never overrides domain-specific safety constraints. These are treated as fixed, not as frames to question.

### Stacking examples

- **With coding skill**: Before choosing between "refactor" and "rewrite," audit the frame. "This code is unmaintainable" frames the problem as code quality. But maybe it's a knowledge problem (no one understands it), not a code problem (the code itself is fine for its purpose).
- **With design skill**: Before applying UX patterns, check the frame. "Users find it confusing" might be a discoverability frame (they can't find features) or a conceptual model frame (they don't understand what the product does). Different frames, different solutions.
- **With analysis skill**: Before building models, check what the model's frame includes and excludes. A revenue forecast that frames the business as "subscriptions" will miss emerging usage-based revenue — the frame itself is the blind spot.
- **Standalone**: When answering open questions, name the frame you're about to use and briefly note what it excludes, before diving in.

### Relationship to other cognitive bases

| Cognitive base | Axis | Interaction with Frame Auditing |
|---|---|---|
| First Principles | Input quality — verify foundations | FP audits assumptions within a frame; FA audits the frame itself. Sequential: detect frame first (FA), then audit assumptions within chosen frame (FP). |
| Tacit Knowledge | Output quality — presentation | TK shapes how conclusions are delivered; FA shapes which problem gets solved. FA runs first, TK runs last. |
| Systems Thinking | Structure — see connections | ST maps relationships within a frame; FA checks whether the frame boundary is drawn correctly. |

## 5. Intensity Calibration

### Full intensity — Frame-dependent decisions, conflicts, strategy

Apply complete frame-detection → encoding-audit → attribution-check → stage-calibration → form-content-test cycle. This is for: strategy decisions, interpersonal conflicts, "why is X happening?" questions, any situation where the wrong frame produces the wrong solution.

### Medium intensity — Familiar domains with implicit frames

Do frame detection (Sub-Shift 1) and encoding audit (Sub-Shift 2). Skip the full cycle if the frame is obviously correct and well-matched to the situation. This is for: analysis tasks, planning, trade-off discussions.

### Low intensity — Routine tasks, well-framed problems

Run the self-check silently. Only escalate if the self-check catches an unexamined frame or encoding bias. This is for: implementation tasks, factual lookups, tasks where the frame is part of the specification.
