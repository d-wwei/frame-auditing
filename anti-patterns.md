# Frame Auditing Anti-Patterns

Detailed reference for detecting and fixing reasoning failures specific to frame auditing.

---

## 1. Frame Blindness

**Pattern**: Performing deep, rigorous analysis within a given frame without ever questioning whether the frame itself is correct. The analysis is internally sound but solves the wrong problem because the frame was never examined.

**Detection**:
- The response dives directly into solution mode without naming what kind of problem this is
- Changing the question's key noun would produce a fundamentally different analysis, but no one checked
- The response is excellent advice for a problem the user might not actually have
- All evidence cited supports the frame; no contradictory evidence is sought

**Fix**: Before deep analysis, spend one sentence naming the frame and what it excludes. "This question frames the issue as [X]. That means we're treating [Y] as fixed and [Z] as irrelevant. Is that correct?"

```
Frame: "How do we reduce customer churn?"
Blind: Analyze churn metrics, identify at-risk segments, build retention campaigns.
Audited: "Churn" frames this as a retention problem. But your data shows 60% of
"churned" users never activated a core feature. This might be an activation problem
disguised as a churn problem. Retention campaigns won't fix it; onboarding redesign
might. Which frame fits the data better?
```

---

## 2. Frame Nihilism

**Pattern**: Recognizing that everything is framed, then concluding that no frame can be trusted and no judgment can be made. This produces intellectual paralysis disguised as sophistication. "Well, it depends on your perspective" becomes the universal non-answer.

**Detection**:
- Every analysis ends with "it depends" without specifying what it depends on
- Multiple frames are listed but none is recommended or ranked
- The response treats frame awareness as the conclusion rather than the beginning of analysis
- Useful judgment is replaced by meta-commentary about the impossibility of judgment

**Fix**: Yes, all analysis is framed. But frames compete on explanatory power — how much evidence they account for, how accurately they predict outcomes. Name multiple frames, then evaluate which one explains more. Frame awareness is a tool for better judgment, not a substitute for it.

```
Nihilist: "Whether this is a good strategy depends on your frame. From a growth
perspective it's good, from a profitability perspective it's bad, from a customer
perspective it's mixed. There's no objective answer."

Audited: "Growth frame says yes, profitability frame says no. But your runway is
14 months and revenue growth is flat — the profitability frame is binding because
it's connected to survival. The growth frame becomes relevant only after unit
economics are positive. Recommendation: profitability frame now, growth frame later."
```

---

## 3. Frame Decoration

**Pattern**: Going through the motions of "switching perspective" or "reframing" but actually producing the same analysis with different vocabulary. The frame shift is cosmetic — the words change, the conclusion doesn't.

**Detection**:
- The "new frame" produces the same recommendation as the original
- Removing the frame-switching language changes nothing about the substance
- The reframe could be generated by find-and-replace on key terms
- The response claims to have "considered multiple perspectives" but all perspectives converge on one answer

**Fix**: A genuine frame switch changes the recommendation, the relevant evidence, or both. If your "reframe" produces the same conclusion, you didn't switch frames — you translated. The test: does the new frame suggest an action the old frame would have rejected?

```
Decorated: "From the customer's perspective, we need better onboarding. From the
business perspective, we also need better onboarding. From the engineering
perspective, onboarding improvements are the priority."

Audited: "Customer frame → onboarding is confusing. Business frame → actually, the
data shows users who complete onboarding still have low retention. The problem might
be downstream of onboarding — the core product loop doesn't create recurring value.
Business frame suggests investigating product-market fit, not polishing onboarding."
```

---

## 4. Frame Equivalence Fallacy

**Pattern**: Treating all frames as equally valid in the name of fairness or open-mindedness. Refusing to rank frames by explanatory power because "who's to say which perspective is right?" This is epistemological cowardice masquerading as intellectual humility.

**Detection**:
- "Both sides have valid points" without distinguishing which points explain more evidence
- Frames are presented as subjective preferences rather than competing explanations
- Evidence quality is ignored in favor of representational balance
- The response would satisfy everyone and inform no one

**Fix**: Frames are not opinions. They are models, and models differ in how much they explain and predict. Compare frames on: (1) how much evidence each accounts for, (2) how accurately each predicts outcomes, (3) how many actionable levers each reveals. The frame that scores higher on these criteria is not "more correct" in some absolute sense — it is more useful.

```
Equivalence: "Some see remote work as a productivity boost, others as a culture
killer. Both perspectives have merit."

Audited: "Remote work's effect depends on the type of work. For independent deep
work (coding, writing, analysis), remote shows 13-20% productivity gains in
controlled studies. For novel cross-functional collaboration, co-location shows
measurable advantages. The 'culture' frame doesn't predict anything specific — it's
too vague. The task-type frame generates testable predictions. Use that one."
```

## 5. Encoding Blindness

**Pattern**: Not noticing that the description of "objective facts" already contains implicit judgments through word choice, emphasis, ordering, and framing. The analyst believes they are being neutral, but their encoding has pre-decided the conclusion.

**Detection**:
- Key claims use evaluative adjectives disguised as descriptive ones ("complex," "aggressive," "innovative," "struggling")
- The same situation could be described with opposite-valence words with equal accuracy
- Ordering or emphasis highlights evidence that supports one interpretation
- The "neutral summary" would be recognized as advocacy by someone with the opposite view

**Fix**: Rewrite the key claim using words that suggest the opposite evaluation. If the rewrite is equally defensible from the evidence, your original encoding was doing hidden argumentative work. Make the judgment explicit instead of embedding it in word choice.

```
Encoded: "The team has been struggling with the legacy codebase and has fallen
behind on the ambitious roadmap."

Decoded: "Struggling" implies the team is the problem. "Ambitious" implies the
roadmap is admirable. Neutral: "The team has completed 40% of planned features.
Contributing factors: codebase complexity (high coupling), roadmap scope (30% larger
than previous quarter), and team capacity (2 of 5 engineers joined last month)."

The neutral version reveals that the roadmap scope and team ramp-up are equal
contributors — the "struggling team" encoding hid those factors.
```

## 6. Attribution Externalization

**Pattern**: Automatically explaining failures, resistance, or poor outcomes by pointing to external factors — the market, competitors, the other team, timing, resources — without checking whether the explainer's own actions contributed.

**Detection**:
- Every failure explanation centers on factors outside the explainer's control
- "We did everything right, but..." appears explicitly or implicitly
- The analysis would change zero behaviors on the explainer's side
- Internal factors are mentioned only as "we didn't have enough [resources/time/support]" — still framing the gap as someone else's responsibility

**Fix**: Before any external attribution, apply Mencius' 反求诸己: name at least one internal factor. Not as self-blame, but as an actionable lever. External causes may be real but are often uncontrollable. Internal causes are often invisible but actionable.

```
External: "The product launch failed because the market wasn't ready, the sales
team didn't understand the product, and the competitor released a similar feature
the same week."

Audited: "Before looking at market timing and sales readiness: Did we validate
market readiness before setting the launch date? Did we train the sales team with
enough lead time? Did we have competitive intelligence on the competitor's roadmap?
The market and the competitor are external. Our launch timing, sales enablement, and
competitive monitoring are internal. Starting with the internal levers: launch date
was set by the board meeting calendar, not by market signals. That's an internal
process problem with a concrete fix."
```

---

## Quick Self-Check Sequence

When reviewing your output for frame auditing anti-patterns, scan in this order:

1. **Opening** → Frame blindness? (Did I name the frame before diving in?)
2. **Conclusion** → Frame nihilism? (Did I actually judge, or did I say "it depends"?)
3. **Reframing** → Frame decoration? (Did switching frames change the recommendation?)
4. **Balance** → Frame equivalence? (Am I treating all frames as equally valid instead of ranking by explanatory power?)
5. **Word choice** → Encoding blindness? (Would opposite-valence words be equally defensible?)
6. **Causation** → Attribution externalization? (Did I check internal causes first?)
